# PHI De-identification Policy and Procedure 

## Purpose 

This policy outlines the procedures for using and disclosing de-identified information, in line with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA). It aims to guide the process of de-identifying Protected Health Information (PHI) to ensure compliance with HIPAA regulations. 

## Scope 

This policy is applicable to all employees (workforce members) and contractors of Graphene. 

## General Rules 

PHI is not subject to the HIPAA Privacy Rule if it has been de-identified according to HIPAA standards. De-identified health information can be used or disclosed without requiring individual authorization. Information is deemed deidentified if: (a) it does not identify an individual; and (b) there is no reasonable way to link it back to an individual. PHI that has not been de-identified according to this policy remains sensitive and must be handled in accordance with Graphene's policies regarding access, maintenance, use, and disclosure. Graphene may use PHI to generate de-identified data for legitimate purposes. 

## Policy and Procedure 

## De-identification of PHI 

Health information is considered de-identified if it does not reveal the identity of an individual or cannot be used to identify an individual. Two primary methods for de-identification are: 

## Statistical Method 

Graphene can determine that health information is not individually identifiable if a qualified expert in statistical or scientific methods: Concludes that the information does not identify an individual. Assesses that the risk of identifying an individual is minimal, even when combined with other available information. Documents the analysis process and its findings to justify the de-identification. The designation of an expert for de-identification does not require specific degrees or certifications, but the individual must possess relevant education and experience in statistics, mathematics, or related fields. Graphene must provide evidence of the expert’s qualifications if requested by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Office for Civil Rights (OCR). 

## Safe Harbor Method 

To de-identify health information using this method, Graphene must remove all 18 identifiers related to individuals, including their relatives, employers, or household members. These identifiers include: 

 Names Geographic subdivisions smaller than a state, such as street addresses, cities, and ZIP codes (except the first three digits of ZIP codes) Dates directly related to individuals, like birth dates or admission dates, and ages over 89 (which can be aggregated into a single category of 90 or older) Telephone numbers Vehicle identifiers, including license plate numbers Fax numbers Device identifiers and serial numbers

## Re-identification of De-identified Information 

Graphene may assign a code or another identification method to re-identify de-identified information, provided that: The code or identification method is not derived from or linked to information about the individual and cannot be used to identify them. Graphene does not use or disclose the code or identification method for other purposes and does not reveal how the re-identification can be achieved. The disclosure of a code or identification method that allows for the re-identification of de-identified data is also considered a PHI disclosure. 

## Related Information 

For further details, refer to the HHS guidance document published in January 2013 titled “Guidance regarding methods for de-identification of Protected Health Information (PHI) in accordance with the HIPAA Privacy Rule.” The document is available at the HHS's page on De-identification Guidance.
